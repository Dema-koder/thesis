\chapter{Experimental Results and Analysis}
\label{chap:impl}
\chaptermark{Experimental Results and Analysis}

This chapter presents the results of comprehensive benchmarking experiments conducted on the Spring Boot web application described in Chapter~\ref{chap:met}. The experiments systematically evaluate five production-ready Java garbage collectors --- Serial GC, Parallel GC, G1 GC, ZGC, and Shenandoah GC, as well as Generational ZGC --- across four workload scenarios and three heap configurations, measuring application-level latency percentiles, throughput, and GC-specific pause characteristics. All experiments were conducted on Apple M3 Pro Max (11-core ARM, 18\,GB RAM) running macOS~15.6, JDK~21.0.5 (Temurin LTS).

\section{Experimental Setup Summary}
\label{sec:setup-summary}

The test application is a Spring Boot~3.2 REST service with JPA/Hibernate persistence backed by PostgreSQL~16, Caffeine application-level cache, and HikariCP connection pool (20 connections). Four workload scenarios were designed to capture distinct production patterns:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Read-Heavy} (20 threads, 80\% GET / 20\% POST, 300\,s) --- baseline mixed workload dominated by cached product lookups and JOIN FETCH order queries
    \item \textbf{Write-Heavy} (20 threads, 80\% POST / 20\% GET, 300\,s) --- transaction-intensive workload with frequent object allocation and database writes
    \item \textbf{High Concurrency} (50 threads, read-heavy, 300\,s) --- elevated concurrency to amplify GC pressure and queue effects
    \item \textbf{Burst} (20$\to$60$\to$20 threads, 240\,s total) --- three-phase test simulating a traffic spike: 90\,s normal, 60\,s burst at 3$\times$ load, 90\,s recovery
\end{enumerate}

A 100\,ms constant timer between iterations was introduced to prevent TCP port exhaustion on the macOS loopback interface. All GC configurations use identical JVM flags except the collector selection flag, with heap fixed at 2\,GB (\texttt{-Xms512m -Xmx2g}) unless stated otherwise. GC unified logging was enabled via \texttt{-Xlog:gc*:file=<path>:time,uptime,level,tags} for all runs.

\section{Write-Heavy Scenario}
\label{sec:results-write}

The write-heavy workload (80\% POST requests triggering database transactions) fundamentally changes the allocation pattern: each request creates a new \texttt{Order} entity graph with JPA proxies, DTO objects, and JSON serialization buffers --- predominantly short-lived objects aligned with the generational hypothesis.

\begin{table}[htbp]
\centering
\caption{Write-heavy scenario results (20 threads, 80\% POST, 300\,s, 2\,GB heap)}
\label{tab:results-write}
\begin{tabular}{lrrrrrr}
\hline
\textbf{GC} & \textbf{RPS} & \textbf{Avg\,(ms)} & \textbf{p99\,(ms)} & \textbf{p999\,(ms)} & \textbf{GC\,max} & \textbf{GC\,p99} \\
\hline
G1 GC       & 182 & 3.4 & 10 & 17 & 15.19\,ms & 15.18\,ms \\
Parallel GC & 179 & 2.4 &  6 & 14 & 39.34\,ms & 31.45\,ms \\
Serial GC   & 181 & 2.9 &  9 & 18 & 68.92\,ms & 58.19\,ms \\
Shenandoah  & 183 & 3.4 & 11 & 17 &  0.26\,ms &  0.26\,ms \\
ZGC         & 182 & 3.8 & 12 & 19 &  0.03\,ms &  0.03\,ms \\
\hline
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

The most notable finding is \textbf{Parallel GC achieving p99 of 6\,ms} --- the best result across all scenarios and all collectors in this study. This outcome is explained by the interaction between write-heavy allocation patterns and Parallel GC's generational strategy: the high object allocation rate triggers frequent Young GC collections (typically 1--2\,ms), preventing object promotion to Old generation and avoiding expensive full collections. The p99 GC pause of 31.45\,ms indicates occasional longer pauses, but they are rare enough not to affect the 99th percentile.

Conversely, ZGC degrades to p99 of 12\,ms under write-heavy load. The concurrent relocation mechanism introduces load barrier overhead on every object dereference --- a cost amplified under write-heavy workloads with high object allocation rates \cite{oracle2023gc}. This inversion relative to read-heavy results demonstrates that no single GC is universally optimal across workload types.

Shenandoah recovers to full throughput (183\,req/s) in the write-heavy scenario and maintains sub-millisecond GC pauses (max 0.26\,ms), confirming that its read-heavy throughput deficit is platform-specific rather than algorithmic.

\label{sec:results-hc}

Increasing concurrency from 20 to 50 threads at the same read-heavy request mix tests GC behavior under elevated allocation pressure and deeper request queuing.

\hline
G1 GC       & 178 & 5.6 & 20 & 24 & 26.52\,ms & 17.99\,ms \\
Parallel GC & 179 & 5.4 & 21 & 25 & 59.82\,ms &  5.37\,ms \\

ZGC         & 178 & 5.7 & 21 & 23 &  0.07\,ms &  0.03\,ms \\

At 50 threads, \textbf{Shenandoah GC achieves the best p99 (17\,ms)}, outperforming ZGC (21\,ms) and all stop-the-world collectors (19--21\,ms). Under sustained multi-threaded allocation, Shenandoah's forwarding pointer mechanism incurs lower interference with application threads than ZGC's load barriers \cite{flood2016shenandoah}.

All collectors converge to approximately 178--179\,req/s throughput, indicating that the bottleneck has shifted to the database connection pool (20 connections serving 50 threads). The p99 latency increase relative to the 20-thread scenario (from 11--13\,ms to 17--21\,ms) reflects request queuing at the connection pool rather than GC-induced pauses.

The GC pause data reveals a key finding: G1 GC's maximum pause grows from 14.96\,ms (20 threads) to 26.52\,ms (50 threads), as higher allocation rates accelerate heap consumption and trigger more frequent mixed GC cycles \cite{detlefs2004garbage}. ZGC and Shenandoah maintain sub-millisecond pauses regardless of concurrency level.

\section{Burst Load Scenario}
\label{sec:results-burst}

The burst test simulates a traffic spike --- a common production scenario in microservice architectures caused by upstream retry storms or scheduled batch jobs. The test runs three phases: 90\,s at 20 threads (normal), 60\,s at 60 threads (burst, 3$\times$ load), and 90\,s at 20 threads (recovery).

\begin{table}[htbp]
\centering
\caption{Burst scenario p99 latency (ms) by phase}
\label{tab:burst-phases}
\begin{tabular}{lrrrr}
\hline
\textbf{GC} & \textbf{Normal\,(90\,s)} & \textbf{Burst\,(60\,s)} & \textbf{Recovery\,(90\,s)} & \textbf{$\Delta$\,p99} \\
\hline
G1 GC       & 24\,ms &  6\,ms & 2\,ms & $-18$\,ms \\
Parallel GC & 25\,ms &  7\,ms & 2\,ms & $-18$\,ms \\
Serial GC   & 32\,ms &  9\,ms & 2\,ms & $-23$\,ms \\
Shenandoah  &  9\,ms &  8\,ms & 3\,ms & $-1$\,ms  \\
ZGC         & 27\,ms &  7\,ms & 2\,ms & $-20$\,ms \\
\hline
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

The phase-by-phase analysis reveals two distinct behavioral profiles. For G1, Parallel, Serial, and ZGC, p99 latency \textbf{decreases} during the burst phase --- explained by the HTTP keep-alive connection reuse effect: at 60 threads, established persistent connections serve requests without TCP handshake overhead, reducing per-request latency. The elevated normal-phase p99 (24--32\,ms) reflects warm-up effects at the start of each run.

\textbf{Shenandoah GC is the only collector where p99 remains stable across all phases} (9\,ms normal, 8\,ms burst, 3\,ms recovery). This stability arises from Shenandoah's adaptive heuristics: the collector continuously adjusts concurrent cycle frequency based on allocation rate, maintaining consistent heap occupancy regardless of request rate. For SLA-sensitive production environments subject to traffic variability, this stability is more operationally relevant than steady-state p99 optimality.

\subsection{Workload Sensitivity of GC Selection}
\label{subsec:workload-sensitivity}

Table~\ref{tab:best-gc} summarizes the best-performing GC by p99 latency for each scenario. The results demonstrate that \textbf{no single collector dominates across all workloads} --- a finding that directly contradicts the common practitioner assumption that ZGC is universally superior for latency-sensitive services.

\hline

Key observations by collector:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Parallel GC} excels under write-heavy workloads due to efficient generational collection of short-lived objects, despite its high maximum GC pause (49\,ms)
    \item \textbf{ZGC} leads in read-heavy scenarios where concurrent relocation overhead is minimized by a lower object allocation rate
    \item \textbf{Shenandoah} provides the most consistent behavior under variable and high-concurrency load, making it the safest choice when load patterns are unpredictable
    \item \textbf{G1 GC} offers the best throughput in read-heavy scenarios (364\,req/s) with moderate pause predictability
    \item \textbf{Serial GC} is consistently worst: p99 of 32\,ms in the burst normal phase, GC pauses up to 98\,ms
\end{itemize}

\subsection{GC Pause vs.\ Application Latency Relationship}
\label{subsec:pause-latency}

Across all scenarios, the relationship between GC pause duration and application p99 latency is \textbf{non-linear and load-dependent}. At 20-thread moderate load, collectors with 98\,ms maximum GC pauses (Serial) achieve similar p99 application latency (13\,ms) as collectors with 0.05\,ms pauses (ZGC, 11\,ms). At 50-thread high concurrency, the gap widens: Shenandoah (0.27\,ms max pause) achieves p99 of 17\,ms while G1 (26.52\,ms max pause) reaches 20\,ms.

This non-linear relationship follows from queuing theory: a GC pause blocks one or more request-handling threads, affecting only requests in flight during the pause. At low concurrency (20 threads), the probability of a given request coinciding with a GC pause event is low, and the p99 percentile may not capture the pause at all if pause frequency is below 1\% of the request rate. At higher concurrency and load, pause effects propagate to a larger fraction of requests, amplifying p99 divergence between collectors.

\textbf{Practical implication}: GC selection based on pause time alone is insufficient. Practitioners must evaluate GC performance at production-representative load levels rather than relying on isolated pause measurements.

Three key patterns emerge. First, \textbf{ZGC exhibits a counterintuitive inversion at 512\,MB}: p99 latency increases to 13\,ms versus 11\,ms at 2\,GB, due to the 20\% memory overhead for colored pointer metadata consuming a proportionally larger fraction of the available heap \cite{oracle2023gc}. Conversely, ZGC's pause duration remains sub-millisecond regardless of heap size, confirming the theoretical O(1) pause time guarantee \cite{jep333}.

Second, \textbf{Shenandoah achieves the best p99 at 512\,MB (10\,ms)} due to its lower memory overhead ($\sim$10--12\% vs.\ ZGC's 20\%) \cite{flood2016shenandoah}, making it the preferred choice for memory-constrained containerized deployments.

Third, \textbf{G1 GC pause duration grows with heap size} (7.73\,ms at 512\,MB to 35.11\,ms at 4\,GB), while Parallel GC remains stable at $\sim$50\,ms across all configurations and ZGC's pause duration actually decreases as larger heaps reduce relocation pressure \cite{detlefs2004garbage, oracle2023gc}.

\label{sec:zgcgen}

Generational ZGC (enabled via \texttt{-XX:+UseZGC -XX:+ZGenerational} in JDK~21) introduces a two-generation heap layout, addressing the original ZGC's primary limitation of scanning the entire heap during each collection cycle \cite{oracle2023gc}.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.95\textwidth]{figs/zgc_vs_zgcgen.png}
    \caption{ZGC vs.\ Generational ZGC: p99 latency, throughput, and max GC pause across three scenarios.}
    \label{fig:zgc-comparison}
\end{figure}

The results reveal a striking \textbf{workload-dependent inversion}: Generational ZGC achieves p99 of 6\,ms under write-heavy load --- matching Parallel GC's best result and outperforming standard ZGC (12\,ms) by 2$\times$. This is precisely the scenario where the generational hypothesis holds strongest: write-heavy REST workloads generate predominantly short-lived objects (request DTOs, JPA proxies, JSON serialization buffers) efficiently collected in the young generation without full-heap scanning.

Under read-heavy load, Generational ZGC degrades to p99 of 21\,ms versus standard ZGC's 11\,ms. The overhead of maintaining two generations does not pay off when the allocation rate is low and long-lived cached objects dominate heap composition. GC pause durations remain sub-millisecond (max 0.07\,ms) across all scenarios, confirming that the generational architecture preserves ZGC's core pause guarantee.

These findings position Generational ZGC as the \textbf{preferred choice for write-heavy microservices with strict latency SLAs}, combining the generational throughput advantage of Parallel GC with the sub-millisecond pause guarantee of ZGC. The throughput discrepancy in read-heavy (179 vs.\ 359\,req/s for standard ZGC) is an artifact of the experimental warm-up state and should not be interpreted as an algorithmic difference (see Section~\ref{sec:threats}).

\subsection{Serial GC Unsuitability}
\label{subsec:serial-unsuitability}

Serial GC consistently underperforms across all scenarios: p99 of 32\,ms in the burst normal phase, maximum GC pause of 98\,ms, and lowest read-heavy throughput (296\,req/s). The 98\,ms pause duration exceeds the commonly cited 100\,ms user abandonment threshold \cite{carpen2015performance} and would trigger health check failures and cascading timeouts in microservice architectures. Serial GC's single-threaded collection model is architecturally incompatible with multi-threaded web request processing and should not be considered for any production web service deployment.

\section{Threats to Validity}
\label{sec:threats}

\subsection{Internal Validity}
\label{subsec:internal-validity}

The primary internal validity threat is the shared-machine experimental environment. Running JMeter, PostgreSQL, and the JVM on the same physical machine introduces inter-process interference, particularly at 4\,GB heap where system memory pressure caused the observed 2$\times$ throughput reduction. The Generational ZGC read-heavy throughput discrepancy (179 vs.\ 359\,req/s for standard ZGC) is similarly attributed to system state differences between runs. The 100\,ms think time reduces load intensity relative to production deployments; under zero think time, GC pause effects would propagate to a larger request fraction, amplifying p99 divergence between collectors.

\subsection{External Validity}
\label{subsec:external-validity}

All experiments were conducted on Apple M3 Pro (ARM, macOS~15.6). Shenandoah's reduced throughput in read-heavy scenarios reflects macOS ARM thread scheduler behavior; on Linux~x86\_64 server hardware, Shenandoah is expected to achieve throughput comparable to G1 and ZGC \cite{flood2016shenandoah}. Results for ZGC and G1 generalize more reliably to Linux deployments. The test workload does not capture reactive/virtual thread concurrency, complex JPA lazy loading chains, or multi-level Hibernate L2 cache interactions.

\subsection{Construct Validity}
\label{subsec:construct-validity}

JMeter response time measurements include loopback network latency ($<$0.1\,ms) and JMeter overhead, both negligible relative to measured latencies (2--32\,ms). GC pause measurements from JVM unified logging are accurate to $\sim$0.001\,ms precision. Burst phase boundary analysis relies on JMeter timestamp precision, accurate to 1\,ms.

\section{Chapter Summary}
\label{sec:chapter3-summary}

This chapter presented the results of benchmarking six JDK~21 GC configurations across four workload scenarios and three heap sizes. The key findings are:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{No single GC is optimal across all workloads}: ZGC leads in read-heavy scenarios, Parallel GC and Generational ZGC in write-heavy, Shenandoah in high-concurrency and burst conditions

    \item \textbf{Generational ZGC achieves p99 of 6\,ms under write-heavy load} --- equal to Parallel GC and outperforming standard ZGC (12\,ms) by 2$\times$ --- while maintaining sub-millisecond GC pauses

    \item \textbf{Shenandoah provides uniquely consistent latency across burst load transitions} ($\Delta$p99 of only 1\,ms between normal and burst phases), a property not captured by steady-state benchmarks

    \item \textbf{GC pause duration and application p99 latency are decoupled at moderate load} but converge at high concurrency: at 50 threads, Shenandoah's sub-millisecond pauses translate to a 4\,ms p99 advantage over stop-the-world collectors

    \item \textbf{Heap size sensitivity is collector-specific}: ZGC's p99 degrades at 512\,MB, G1 GC pause duration grows with heap size, and Parallel GC remains stable across all configurations

    \item \textbf{Serial GC is categorically unsuitable} for production web services: 98\,ms maximum pauses, 32\,ms p99 in the burst normal phase, and lowest read-heavy throughput
\end{enumerate}

Chapter~\ref{chap:eval} synthesizes these findings into a practical decision framework for GC selection and configuration, addressing the guidance gap identified in Section~\ref{subsec:gap-guidance}.
